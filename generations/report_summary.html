High-Resolution Image Reconstruction with Latent Diffusion Models from Human Brain Activity

Summary of the paper

This paper presents a new method based on a latent diffusion model (LDM) termed Stable Diffusion to reconstruct high-resolution images with high semantic fidelity from fMRI brain activity. It does not require any additional training or fine-tuning of complex deep-learning models and provides a quantitative interpretation of different LDM components from a neuroscientific perspective. It is applied to encode models, which are predictive models of brain activity built out of features extracted from different components of deep-learning models, and the Natural Scenes Dataset. It is evaluated using perceptual similarity metrics (PSMs) and human raters, and results show that images reconstructed using zc generate high-resolution images with high semantic fidelity. The study also attempts to interpret the internal operations of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDM) models by mapping them to brain activity, and layer-wise characterization of U-Net is performed. Finally, a voxel-wise encoding model is applied to held-out test images in a single subject to compare the prediction performance of the original images and noise-added latent representation. The results show that semantic content emerges throughout the inverse diffusion process, and that the noise-added latent representation predicts activity within higher visual cortex better than the original images.
